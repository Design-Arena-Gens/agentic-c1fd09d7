[CHAPTER 16: COULD THIS HAPPEN TO YOU?]

Here's a question worth asking: Could you accidentally be this neighbor?

And when you hire contractors to do work on your property, you're trusting them to know and follow all the regulations.

But sometimes contractors cut corners. Sometimes they don't pull proper permits. Sometimes they don't check easements or setbacks. Sometimes they just do what the homeowner asks without flagging potential code violations.

And as the property owner, you're ultimately responsible. "I didn't know" isn't a defense. "The contractor said it was fine" doesn't protect you from enforcement. If there's a violation on your property, it's your problem to fix.

So what can you do to protect yourself?

First, always pull permits for major projects. Yes, it's a hassle. Yes, it costs money. Yes, it means inspectors coming to check the work. But permits exist for a reason. They ensure work is done safely and legally. And they protect you from liability later.

Second, get a current property survey before doing any major construction near property lines or in your yard. Don't guess where your boundaries are. Don't assume that old fence is on the actual property line. Pay for a professional survey. It's way cheaper than having to tear down or move something later.

Third, specifically check for utility easements. They should be on your property deed and on any survey. Make sure you and your contractor know exactly where they are. And don't build anything permanent over them. Ever.

Fourth, don't hire contractors who pressure you to skip permits or who say "the city doesn't need to know about this." That's a massive red flag. Professional contractors know that cutting corners on permits and codes can come back to haunt everyone involved.

Fifth, do your own research. Don't just blindly trust contractors or assume everything will be fine. Look up your local building codes. Call your city's planning department with questions. Read your HOA rules if you have one. Being informed protects you.

The neighbor in this story could have avoided disaster with just a few hours of research and a few hundred dollars for a proper survey and permits. Instead, he lost forty thousand dollars.
